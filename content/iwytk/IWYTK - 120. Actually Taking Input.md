Some people are really bad at taking "feedback" and "constructive criticism" and they will tell you so. These tend to be lumped into two groups:

1. Those who feel they are so good, so mature, etc. that there is nothing significant they can be taught. They may be insulted that you would even suggest they might be lacking in some area by offering feedback.
2. Those who are humble enough to recognize that they are good and yet always have room to grow. Who recognize that they often react defensively when someone provides such feedback, but who take the time to process what is shared - even if their initial reaction is sharply negative.

Then there are those who are good at "feedback" and "constructive criticism" and will tell you so. These also tend to be lumped into two groups:

3. Those who believe they are good at taking feedback but actually decline it - sometimes ferociously, sometimes by not taking time to consider it deeply.
4. Those who are actually good at taking feedback - who are able to listen, able to quiet their wounds, and consider deeply what is shared.

If one can be #4 I commend that approach, but for most I think #2 is the most realistic path (and I include myself in that number).

How do you know if you are evaluating yourself honestly? If you are deeply considering the feedback given you?

I'd suggest a few metrics:
1. Do people often come to you with "feedback" of significance - not just minor tweaks here or there?
2. Can you name times when people have come to you with significant feedback that has resulted in measurable changes in your life? How often?
3. How do you handle feedback from trusted individuals when you don't believe it?

There are a few ways I attempt to implement this in my own life. One of the most important is taking the pragmatic advice when the consequences are fairly neutral.

For example, if someone tells me I am angry too often and suggests I read a book - if I trust this individual I am likely to take the book...and read it. It might take me a while, it might not feel true at first, sometimes it still doesn't feel true at the end - but I've spent time with it. Deeply invested myself in understanding and appreciating the feedback I've received.

One area this did occur to me in is going on an Autoimmune Protocol Diet (AIP). I was skeptical about the potential for it to significantly change my life but I also saw that while it demanded some time, it wasn't going to hurt me. So I tried and it did.
