I'm fairly lucky to be alive. I've had more near misses than I can easily remember or count. I enjoy telling the comedic stories of my misadventures and yet they could often have gone much more wrongly or they have in actuality cost me dearly.

There is a balance to be struck in life. We cannot live in constant fear for ourselves or others of what may happen - we will never live our lives. Our indecision results in a decision - a decision to remain rooted in a single place / mindset - and this is in its a severe misadventure with tragic consequences.

We accept that we are but a speck in the universe, that natural and man-made disasters occur with astonishing frequency. We recognize that fearing everything doesn't get us anywhere and so we live without fearing everything.

At the same time we exercise reasonable caution in our lives. For me this is an almost mathematical formula:

*If I do x the chances of y happening are z.*

For example, "If I walk across a busy street without looking both ways the chances of me being struck and seriously injured are 50%."

I then ask myself a few questions:
1. How important is it that I walk across the street without looking both ways? The answer is, "Not at all important." It requires minimal time and energy and exponentially reduces the likelihood that I will be injured.
2. Is the likelihood that I will be hurt high enough that I should intentionally avoid being hit? And here the answer is yes. If the chance of being hit was 1/100,000,000 then perhaps I would choose not to look both ways - but it is a 1/2 chance, so I choose to look.

Lets consider another example. "If I fly in an airplane the likelihood of me dying during the flight is 1/11,000,000." Now let us ask ourselves the questions:
1. How important is it that I fly? The answer may range from "not at all" to "life saving." One might also ask, "Is there another transportation option that would be safer?"
2. Is the likelihood that I will die high enough that I should intentionally avoid flying? Here the answer is (at least for me) no. It is highly unlikely I'll die in an airplane crash.

Now lets consider a different example that deals with more weighty matters:

"There is a child being kidnapped in front of my eyes. Should I intervene and tackle the kidnapper?"

I ask myself the questions:
1. How important is it that I intervene? Highly. A child's life will be destroyed and their death is likely if I choose to stand by.
2. Is the likelihood of my getting hurt high enough that I should avoid intervening?

This time our second question is much more difficult to answer. There is a high likelihood we may be injured or killed in an attempt to intervene. If we look at things purely statistically and from a perspective of self-preservation we might choose not to intervene.

I plan on and hope that I would choose to intervene. That even if I knew my death was a certainty in the situation that I would choose to intervene. Why? Because some things are worth more than life.
