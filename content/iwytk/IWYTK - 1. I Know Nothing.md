> [!QUOTE]
> "I know I know nothing" - Socrates

Socrates didn't quite say that, he said:
> [!QUOTE]
> "I am wiser than this man; for neither of us really knows anything fine and good, but this man thinks he knows something when he does not, whereas I, as I do not know anything, do not think I do either."

Check out [Plato's *Apology* 21](https://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus%3Atext%3A1999.01.0170%3Atext%3DApol.%3Apage%3D21)  for the complete context. 

I've taken this as a dictum for much of my life. I have learned much in my life (thus far) and I have had a variety of experiences but what I know and what I have experienced is strongly constrained by my finitude and my subjectivity.

So when you read IWYTK the most important thing for you to know is that **I, Dave Mackey, know nothing.**

What does this mean for someone's life practically? It means that we attempt to approach life with humility and openness. *I think x is true but I will humbly consider y*.

This does not mean that we must walk around feeling constantly unsure of and unmoored from reality. Rather, we form opinions but we hold them rather loosely. We know what we think is true and yet we are open to learning new truth.

I have frequently heard in the IT discipline I have worked in for most of my life the principle "Strong opinions, loosely held."[^1] This is extremely important for us in IT - we work within a context in which knowledge and best practices are constantly changing and evolving. We work with what we know and build the best we can while also remaining open to the possibility, perhaps even probability, that there are ways in which our practice can improve.

**Takeaway**:
Don't be afraid to live by what you "know" but be humble enough to truly listen and consider the "truth" as expressed by others - even (perhaps especially) if it differs significantly from your own.

You don't have to accept others "truth" but you will be enriched by attempting to understand it.

[^1]: This appears to have originated with Paul Saffo.
