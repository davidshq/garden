Lots of people will want to give you advice. They'll tell you things. Some will be true, some will be false. How do you know what to believe and from whom to take advice?

It is important that you have a sense of self that you trust. This is who you say you are.

I have OCD so sometimes I can't even trust that "self". Or more accurately, I need to differentiate between my OCD self and my real self. OCD is an illness that makes me suffer, my real self is valid and valuable.

So we want to take advice from ourselves, to follow our own inner truth, but we also have to mind that our inner truth can be deceived, overwhelmed, distorted.

In general, our inner truth should lead us (over the long-term) towards sustainability (the ability to keep doing life), happiness (a sense of positivity about our place and contribution in the world), humility (an accurate, not hateful understanding of ourselves), and love (the bringing of good to others and the receiving of good from others).

We also want to take advice from others - recognizing that their inner truths suffer from the same deficits as ours. Even well-intentioned people have their weak spots. So which do we take advice from?

Those we trust, those who have shown us who they are as they have lived life alongside us, and those who have specialized in a specific area and deserve our trust.

I try to take what anyone says to me humbly. If someone tells me something about myself that is negative, I try to consider it. But I also factor how much deliberation I place upon myself based on the relationship I have with this individual. If they are someone whom I know and trust, who loves me, and whom I know is looking out for my best interest I'll spend a lot more time attempting to understand and integrate (as appropriate) what they have said than a coworker who is always trying to get a promotion by putting others down or a boss who is so focused on making money that they don't care for the well-being of their employees.

It is also wise to take advice from those who are experienced in a given area, have studied a specific topic, etc. It is not that we trust them without reserve but that we seriously consider and evaluate what they say and often implement their recommendations.
