# Think Differently

## Introduction
This section includes materials which have impacted the way I think, particularly by challenging or informing my understanding of the world.

## Cautionary Note:
These works have been read over a period of 30+ years, many of them had an impact on me but I cannot vouch for their accuracy after so many years. If you find any contain misinformation that impacts the worth of the work significantly, please let me know.
## Fiction
- Philip Pullman. His Dark Materials Trilogy.
	- Written partially as a counter-series to C.S. Lewis' Chronicles of Narnia.
- Philip K. Dick. The VALIS Trilogy.
	- One of Dick's clearest attempts to make sense of reality.
- Aldous Huxley. Brave New World.
	- I know, I know, I'm wrong - but I prefer Huxley over Orwell for classic dystopia.
- George MacDonald. Lilith.
	- A gothic novel with complex allegorical elements.
- Anita Diamant. The Red Tent.
- Steve Alten. Meg.
	- Okay, this is mainly for fun.
- Fyodor Dostoevsky. Crime and Punishment.
- Jeff Shaara. The Civil War Trilogy.
	- A three-volume novelization of the American Civil War. It needs to be read alongside Jeff Shaara's novels on the American Revolution and as such provides some interesting insight into character and behavior during both conflicts.
- Hank Hanegraaff and Sigmund Brouwer. The Last Disciple Series.
	- Offers a preterist view of the apocalyptic scriptures in the Christian New Testament through a novelization. Challenges popular (Left Behind) evangelical thinking on the meaning of the Book of Revelation, etc.
## History
- H.W. Brands. The Money Men: Capitalism, Democracy, and the Hundred Years' War Over the American Dollar.
- Vicki León. Uppity of Women of Medieval Times.
	- Short biographical entries on various amazing women who lived and did things during the male-dominated medieval ages.
- Fawn M. Brodie. No Man Knows My Story.
	- A biography of Joseph Smith, founder of Mormonism. Provides fascinating insights into the relatively recent development of a religion and the historical context in which Mormonism arose. I find it most useful in regard to these two areas. Helping me understand:
1. How can a religion come into being?
2. What was the world like in the 19th century?
- James Reston Jr. Warriors of God: Richard the Lionheart and Saladin in the Third Crusade.
- Julian Baggini. Great Thinkers A-Z.
- Roger Crowley. 1453: The Holy War for Constantinople and the Clash of Islam and the West.
- Anthony Summers. Official and Confidential: The Secret Life of J. Edgar Hoover.
- Patricia C. Click. Time Full of Trial: The Roanoke Island Freedmen's Colony, 1862-1867.
- Hunter S. Thompson. Hell's Angels: A Strange and Terrible Saga.
- Douglas Preston. The Monster of Florence.
- Linus Torvalds. Just for Fun: The Story of an Accidental Revolutionary.
- John Howard Griffin. Black Like Me.
- Garth Lean. On the Tail of a Comet: The Life of Frank Buchman.
- Alvin S. Felzenberg. The Leaders We Deserved (and a Few We Didn't): Rethinking the Presidential Ranking Game.
- Catherine Clinton. Mrs. Lincoln: A Life.
- Hallie Rubenhold. The Lady in Red: An Eighteenth-Century Tale of Sex, Scandal, and Divorce.
- James Gleick. Isaac Newton.
	- I actually wasn't a huge fan of this biography, but it's the only one on Newton I've read thus far.
- Johan Huizinga. Erasmus and the Age of Reformation.
	- There are probably better books out there on Erasmus, but again, this is the only one I've read.
