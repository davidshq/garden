# Core Reading

## Introduction
What I have read has played a significant role in forming who I am today - and not always for the better. In this section I provide a list of reading materials that I consider formative to my development and which I would recommend to you.

I hope you'll share with me the written words that have been most impactful in your life.

## Getting Things Done (David Allen)
- David Allen. [Getting Things Done: The Art of Stress-Free Productivity](https://amzn.to/40dK6H4).

I can't say I'm a guru at productivity, I have ADHD and that plays havoc with my capabilities - but learning the system outlined by Allen in this book helped me develop better sytsems for myself.

I don't follow GTD religiously, but I have integrated what works for me into my own custom system (which attempts to account for the variable energy and focus that both ADHD and chronic illness cause).

## Against Depression (Peter D. Kramer)
- Peter D. Kramer. [Against Depression](https://amzn.to/4lGhgXS).

I remember picking this book up in the Langhorne (Pennsylvania) Public Library. I struggled for much of my life with major depressive disorder (and still struggle with depression) and I was always desperately looking for a way out.

I picked up this book thinking that it would be about how to fight depression - but it was about something infinitely more important. Dr. Kramer attacks the concept of depression as caused by the weakness, moral failure, or artistic/creative essence that often is associated with it. He demonstrates how the body is physically damaged by depression.

It is an important work in finding one's way out from underneath the societal lies that often burden those struggling with depression.

Kramer has written a number of books since, I have not yet had the pleasure of reading them. It is likely that he has written a more updated and illuminary volume since.

## Eat to Live (Joel Fuhrman)
- Joel Fuhrman. [Eat to Live: The Revolutionary Formula for Fast and Sustained Weight Loss](https://amzn.to/3VntTMF).

Dr. Fuhrman's book came to me at an odd time in my life. While I've always struggle with depression, ADHD, OCD, and so on, I was physically rather healthy and active. I went to my GP and asked what the next step would be to improve my health, he recommended this book.

Ironically, after I read the book (but before making any really significant changes to implement it) my health took a precipitous dive off a cliff. It wouldn't be until years later that a friend ([Sarah Matta](https://www.helloiamprogrammer.com/)) would take the time to walk me through the process of the Autoimmune Protocol (AIP) through which I experienced significant relief (though by no means cessation) of many of my health issues.

Fuhrman's book, while not AIP, shares many important concepts with AIP and is not as extreme as AIP. I've finished working through it a second time and have now begun his more recent (and probably to be recommended over Eat to Live), Eat for Life.

NOTE: The importance of community and support, imho, cannot be overemphasized in the movement towards better health. I had long wanted to move into healthier eating but found the task impossible. It was only when someone walked with me through it that I was able to make the change. I think education is essential but camaderie is (at least for some of us) fundamental.

## Hold Me Tight (Sue Johnson)
- Sue Johnson. [Hold Me Tight: Seven Conversations for a Lifetime of Love](https://amzn.to/4fWmxJI).
An invaluable book on healthy partner relationships. Written by the founder of EFT it provides a convincing explanation of why couples end up in conflict and how to stop the spiral.

## Feeling Good (David D. Burns)
- David D. Burns. [Feeling Good: The New Mood Therapy](https://amzn.to/4fQmW0l).
I wish there was a more concise book on this topic I liked as much, but I haven't found it yet. Burns is a bit wordy but has excellent worksheets and processes throughout the book for dealing with all sorts of psychological struggles - depression, anxiety, relational, etc.

## A Rulebook for Arguments (Anthony Weston)
- Anthony Weston. [A Rulebook for Arguments](https://amzn.to/4lH1njO).
An extremely thin little volume which helps expose a number of logical fallacies we and others often use when discussing/arguing a topic.

## Co-Dependence: Misunderstood--Mistreated (Anne Wilson Schaef)
- Anne Wilson Schaef. [Co-Dependence: Misunderstood--Mistreated](https://amzn.to/3Hq3zOY).

A slim volume that some people "click" with and others ask me, "Why in the world am I reading this?" It is somewhat dry and at first can seem a bit unapplicable but what I love about the book is how Schaef demonstrates so clearly that our doing and being "good" can arise from a pathology which negates our behavior, in fact causes it to be "wrong" (damaging).

## Sex in the Forbidden Zone (Peter Rutter)
- Peter Rutter. [Sex in the Forbidden Zone: When Men in Power--Therapists, Doctors, Clergy, Teachers, and Others--Betray Women's Trust](https://amzn.to/3Vgqmjj).

This is another one of those books, like Schaef's above that people either "get" or "don't." It is aged and deals primarily with the power dynamics inherent in various relationships (counselor/client, doctor/patient, pastor/parishioner), especially as they relate to male/female sexual relations.

It is valuable on this level but it is also quite valuable for its insights into the nature of the underlying psychology and pathology which often form attractive relationships with a sexual potential.
